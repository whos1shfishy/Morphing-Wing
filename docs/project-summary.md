# Project Summary

The Flying Fish is a sliding-mechanism morphing wing developed as part of a UNSW DESN1000 engineering design project. The goal was to create a wing that can dynamically modify its camber through a morphing trailing edge, improving aerodynamic performance compared with a fixed solid airfoil.

The wing is based on a NACA 0015 profile with a 150 mm chord and 90 mm span. The internal structure houses a custom bilateral rack-and-pinion mechanism driven by two SG90 servos, one on each side of the wing. These servos pull a sliding trailing-edge section to achieve continuous camber change.

Early prototypes used a single central servo and a single rack-and-pinion system, which demonstrated that the concept worked but also revealed asymmetric deflection, twisting under load, and high torque on the lone actuator. The final Flying Fish design transitioned to a dual-servo configuration with mirrored racks on both sides of the trailing edge.

The final implementation uses a three-piece 3D-printed structure (central spine plus two side shells) and, in later versions, PETG material for increased flexibility. This architecture aims to keep the part count and weight low while making assembly, maintenance, and iteration straightforward.
